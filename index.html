Then Vs Now: UW - Madison

"The picture of it stays, and not just in my rememory, but out there, in the world."
Sethe — Toni Morrison, Beloved
Created By Charlie Van Lyssel

In Beloved, Sethe explains the concept of "rememory" as something that lives outside the mind, and rather in the world

This project is a walk through seven places on the UW–Madison campus. All seven of these locations represent how a place can exist in the world across generations, and change over time.

I

Bascom Hall

Top of Bascom Hill · Built 1859
Then · c. 1910
Bascom Hall with its original dome. UW Archives.
Now
Bascom Hill in the modern day.

For over 50 years, Bascom Hall had a tall dome that stood out over the city. A fire destroyed it on October 10, 1916. The university talked for years about rebuilding it, but it never happened. Bascom Hall has now gone longer without the dome than it ever had it, even though old postcards still show the original version.

"Some things you forget. Other things you never do. Places, places are still there." Sethe, Beloved
II

Memorial Union Terrace

Lake Mendota Shore · Opened 1928
Then · 1930s
The Terrace opened to honor WWI veterans.
Now
Memorial Union Terrace in the modern day.

Before Memorial Union was built, this shore was already part of Teejop, the Ho Chunk homeland. UW says this land has been Ho Chunk land since time immemorial. Memorial Union opened on October 5, 1928, as a memorial to UW men and women who served in the nation’s wars. The Terrace opened right after, and the famous sunburst chairs came later.

"Not a house in the country ain't packed to its rafters with some dead Negro's grief." Baby Suggs, Beloved
III

Camp Randall

Monroe Street · Civil War Camp, 1861–65
Then · 1860s
Camp Randall when it served as a Union training camp
Now
Camp Randall in modern day.

Camp Randall sent about 70,000 Union soldiers into the Civil War. Many never made it home. In 1862, the site also held Confederate prisoners, and about 140 of them died and were buried at Forest Hill Cemetery in a section called Confederate Rest. The university later took over the land and built Camp Randall Stadium there in 1917.

"Disremembered and unaccounted for, she cannot be lost because no one is looking for her." Beloved, Beloved
IV

Library Mall

Lower Campus · Reshaped Many Times
Then · c. 1950
Library Mall before the Fountain was built.
Now
Library mall in the modern day

The area south of Bascom Hill has not stayed the same for long. This part of campus has been rebuilt again and again. Memorial Library opened in 1953. Humanities came in 1969, and the university now plans to demolish it. The Mall has had several different lives.

"If a house burns down, it's gone, but the place — the picture of it — stays." Sethe, Beloved
V

The Red Gym

Langdon Street · Built 1894 as an Armory
Then · c. 1900
The Armory and Gymnasium right after construction.
Now
The Red Gym in Modern Day.

TThe Red Gym was built to be both a gym and an armory. After the Haymarket Riot in Chicago, Wisconsin legislators wanted a building in Madison that could help respond to unrest. That is why it was designed like a fortress. The turrets and towers are still there. The rifles are not. Now students go there for IDs and campus services instead.

"We flesh; flesh that weeps, laughs; flesh that dances on bare feet in grass. Love it. Love it hard." Baby Suggs, Beloved
VI

Science Hall

North Park Street · Rebuilt 1887
Then · c. 1888
Science Hall shortly after it was rebuilt following the 1884 fire that destroyed the original.
Now
Modern Day Science Hall.

This is not the first Science Hall. The original building burned in December 1884. The one that replaced it was built with fire in mind, using metal and masonry instead of the older design. It feels like the building still remembers what happened before it was built.

"Anything dead coming back to life hurts." Amy Denver, Beloved
VII

State Street

From Library Mall to the Square
Then · c. 1925
State Street in the 1920s, when streetcars were allowed.
Now
State street in the modern day.

State Street has always linked the university to the rest of Madison. Streetcars once ran down it, and during the Dow protests in October 1967, police rushed through this part of campus and used tear gas at the Commerce Building. Later, the same street held marches, vigils, parades, markets, and all the ordinary days in between. The Capitol has watched over it the whole time.

"It was not a story to pass on." Narrator, Beloved

Reflection

I chose this project because it fits with my skillset of studying cartography and computer science. I felt like the evolution of the UW - Madison campus relates to Beloved as the past of the campus still affects what the buildings are used today. Combining these 2 topics together gives me a strong vision of how history can impact many people for generations in the future.

The hardest part of this project for me was planning out how to organize the webpage to be easy to read and send an effective message to the reader. It was also challenging to find some of the history about the campus, especially buildings that were built in the 1800’s. Lastly, finding a way to connect my topic to Beloved was initially challenging, but became more clear as I worked throughout the project.

What I am happiest about is how the website formatting turned out. It took a lot of work and fine tuning and I was able to get the theme that I was looking for. I really enjoyed researching and finding old pictures of campus and comparing to how they look in the modern day.
